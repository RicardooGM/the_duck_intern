What is Investment Banking?

A ground-up introduction to one of the most sought-after careers in finance — what banks do, how deals get made, and what analysts actually spend their time on.

What is Investment Banking?

Investment banking is a segment of the financial services industry that assists corporations, governments, and other institutions in raising capital and executing complex financial transactions. Unlike retail or commercial banking, which serves individuals and businesses through deposits and loans, investment banks operate primarily in capital markets — facilitating the issuance of securities, advising on mergers and acquisitions, and providing strategic financial counsel to large organisations.

At its core, investment banking exists to solve a fundamental problem: entities with large capital needs — a corporation seeking to expand, a government funding infrastructure, or a private equity firm acquiring a business — require sophisticated financial intermediaries to structure, price, and execute those transactions efficiently.

Key distinction

Investment banks do not take deposits or make retail loans. They earn revenue primarily through advisory fees, underwriting commissions, and trading activities — not the traditional interest income model of commercial banks.

What Do Investment Banks Actually Do?

The activities of an investment bank can be broadly grouped into three categories: capital raising, advisory services, and sales & trading.

Capital Raising involves helping clients access funding through the issuance of equity (stocks) or debt (bonds). When a company conducts an Initial Public Offering (IPO), an investment bank underwrites the offering — meaning it purchases the securities from the issuer and sells them to institutional investors, bearing the price risk in between.

Advisory Services cover mergers, acquisitions, restructurings, and divestitures. When one company seeks to acquire another, investment banks advise on valuation, deal structure, negotiation strategy, and execution. This is commonly referred to as M&A advisory.

Sales & Trading involves buying and selling securities on behalf of clients or the bank itself, providing liquidity to capital markets and generating revenue through bid-ask spreads and proprietary positions.

ECM (Equity Capital Markets) IPOs, follow-on offerings, convertibles Underwriting fees
DCM (Debt Capital Markets) Bond issuance, leveraged loans Underwriting fees

Sales & Trading Market-making, client execution Spreads, commissions

The Main Divisions

A typical investment bank is organised into several distinct divisions, each with a specific function and client base.

Investment Banking Division (IBD) is what most people think of when they hear "investment banking." IBD covers M&A advisory and capital markets transactions. Analysts and associates in IBD build financial models, prepare pitch books, and work directly on live deals.

Sales & Trading (S&T) operates on the trading floor, executing orders for institutional clients and providing market-making services across equities, fixed income, currencies, and commodities.

Research produces equity and credit research reports for institutional investors. Research analysts develop deep expertise in specific sectors or companies and publish investment recommendations.

Asset Management manages investment portfolios on behalf of institutional and high-net-worth clients. Some large banks house significant asset management arms alongside their investment banking operations.

For the intern

When people refer to "breaking into investment banking," they almost always mean joining the Investment Banking Division (IBD) as an analyst. This is the most common entry point for graduates and the focus of most recruiting processes.

The Deal Process

Understanding how a deal progresses from inception to close is essential for any aspiring investment banker. While every transaction is unique, most M&A deals follow a broadly consistent sequence of stages.

The process typically begins with the origination phase, where bankers identify potential transactions and pitch their services to prospective clients. This involves significant relationship-building and requires bankers to have a thorough understanding of their clients' strategic objectives.

Once mandated, the bank enters the execution phase. For a sell-side M&A process, this involves preparing marketing materials (the Confidential Information Memorandum, or CIM), identifying and approaching potential buyers, managing the due diligence process, and negotiating transaction terms.

The final stage is closing — obtaining regulatory approvals, finalising legal documentation, and transferring ownership. From mandate to close, a typical M&A transaction takes between three and twelve months.

Bulge Brackets vs Boutiques

Investment banks are commonly categorised by size, deal focus, and client base. The two primary categories are bulge bracket banks and boutique advisory firms.

Bulge bracket banks — including Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, and Bank of America — are large, full-service institutions with global presence. They advise on the largest transactions, maintain significant trading operations, and employ thousands of bankers across multiple divisions and geographies.

Elite boutiques — such as Lazard, Evercore, Centerview, and PJT Partners — focus exclusively on advisory services, with no trading or commercial banking operations. Despite their smaller size, elite boutiques frequently advise on the largest and most complex M&A transactions, competing directly with bulge brackets for mandates.

Middle market boutiques serve mid-sized companies and focus on transactions typically below $1 billion in value. They offer a more generalised deal exposure and are often considered strong training grounds for analysts.

Life as an IB Analyst

The investment banking analyst role is widely regarded as one of the most demanding entry-level positions in finance. Analysts typically work between 80 and 100 hours per week, with hours being particularly intense during live deal periods.

The work itself is predominantly model-building and document preparation. Analysts spend a significant portion of their time constructing financial models — DCF analyses, LBO models, and merger consequence analyses — as well as preparing pitch books, management presentations, and deal marketing materials.

Despite the demanding hours, the analyst programme is valued for the breadth of experience it provides. Analysts are exposed to a wide range of industries and transaction types, develop strong financial modelling skills, and build a professional network that serves them throughout their career.

Typical analyst day

A first-year analyst might spend the morning updating a DCF model, the afternoon revising slides for a pitch book, and the evening responding to comments from a senior banker on a live deal — often late into the night.

Key Terms to Know

Pitch Book
A presentation prepared by investment bankers to win a mandate or present a transaction recommendation to a client. Typically includes market analysis, valuation, and strategic rationale.
Mandate
The formal engagement of an investment bank by a client to advise on or execute a transaction. Winning a mandate is the result of a competitive pitching process.
Underwriting
The process by which an investment bank purchases securities from an issuer and resells them to investors, guaranteeing the issuer a certain amount of capital.
Buy-side vs Sell-side
Investment banks operate on the sell-side — they advise and facilitate transactions. Buy-side firms (hedge funds, private equity, asset managers) invest capital. Many analysts transition from sell-side to buy-side after two to three years.
League Tables
Rankings of investment banks by transaction volume or value. Published by data providers such as Dealogic and Bloomberg, league tables are a key measure of a bank's market position.

Common Misconceptions

"Investment bankers invest money." They do not. Investment bankers advise clients on transactions and raise capital — they are intermediaries, not investors. The investing is done by the bank's clients.

"You need a finance degree to break in." While a finance or economics background is common, investment banks recruit from a wide range of academic disciplines. What matters more is demonstrable analytical ability, an understanding of markets, and strong performance at a target university.

"The work is mostly relationship-driven." At the junior level, the work is almost entirely analytical — financial modelling, document preparation, and due diligence. Relationship management becomes more prominent at the vice president and managing director levels.

The Bottom Line

Investment banking occupies a central role in the global financial system, facilitating the capital allocation decisions of corporations, governments, and institutions. For those considering a career in finance, it represents one of the most technically rigorous and professionally rewarding entry points available.

Understanding what investment banks do — and what they do not do — is the first step toward navigating the recruiting process with clarity and purpose.
